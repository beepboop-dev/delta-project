As a freelancer, contracts are your lifeline. They protect your payment, define your work, and set boundaries. But a bad contract can cost you thousands, steal your IP, or lock you out of future work. Here are the 10 clauses you must check every time.

The 10-Point Freelance Contract Checklist

1. Scope of Work

The most important clause. It should clearly define what you're delivering, how many revisions are included, and what's NOT included (scope creep protection).

2. Payment Terms

When do you get paid? How? What about late payments?

  • Always get a deposit (25-50% upfront is standard)
  • Net-15 or Net-30 max (avoid Net-60+)
  • Include late payment penalties (1.5%/month is standard)
  • Specify currency and payment method
üö© Red Flag: "Payment upon project completion" with no milestones ‚Äî you could work for months with no pay.

3. Kill Fee / Cancellation Clause

What happens if they cancel mid-project? You should be compensated for work already done, plus a cancellation fee (typically 25-50% of remaining value).

4. Intellectual Property

This is where freelancers get burned the most. Common approaches:

  • License: You keep ownership, they get usage rights (best for freelancers)
  • Transfer on payment: IP transfers when you're fully paid (good compromise)
  • Work for hire: They own everything from the start (worst for freelancers)
üö© Red Flag: "All work product, including preliminary drafts, is work made for hire" ‚Äî you can't even use it in your portfolio.

5. Non-Compete Clause

Some clients try to prevent you from working with competitors. As a freelancer, this can devastate your business.

üö© Red Flag: Any non-compete lasting more than 6 months or covering a broad industry.

6. Confidentiality

Reasonable confidentiality is fine. Overbroad confidentiality that prevents you from mentioning you worked with the client is not.

7. Liability Cap

Your liability should be capped at the amount they paid you. Never accept unlimited liability.

8. Revision Limits

Unlimited revisions = unlimited free work. Always specify a number (2-3 rounds is standard) with additional revisions billed hourly.

9. Termination Clause

Both parties should be able to terminate with reasonable notice (14-30 days). Ensure you're paid for work completed.

10. Portfolio Rights

Make sure you can show the work in your portfolio. If there's a confidentiality clause, carve out an exception for portfolio use.
